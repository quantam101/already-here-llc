## LinkedIn #2 — pricing transparency (week 2)

We did something field-service companies almost never do: we published
every price.

All three retainer tiers. The full standalone rate card. All 42 productized
services with fixed scopes and deliverables.

Why? Because "it depends" is why field work gets deferred until something
breaks. When a rack audit is $200/rack and a POS lane deployment is $150,
procurement can approve it without a quote cycle — and we stop spending
sales calls on work we could be doing.

What we learned pricing 42 services:
1. Fixed scope forces honest scope. Writing "what's excluded" is harder
   than writing "what's included" — and more valuable.
2. Naming the deliverable (report + photos, port map, signed checklist)
   ends the "is it done?" debate before it starts.
3. Transparent overage rates make retainers trustworthy. Ours drop from
   $100/hr to $90/hr as tiers go up, in writing.

The whole catalog is public on our site. Steal the format for your own
services — transparency compounds.

## LinkedIn #3 — the capacity argument (week 3)

"$2,000 a month buys 30 hours" is how field retainers get sold — and it's
the wrong frame on both sides.

That's $66/hour. After travel, scheduling, insurance, and non-billable
time, a provider at that rate either cuts corners or quietly disappears
when you actually need them.

What a retainer should buy is **reserved capacity**:
→ Guaranteed response windows (ours: 24–48hrs, with service credits if missed)
→ Scheduling priority when everyone's calendar is on fire
→ A defined block of hours for break/fix and smart hands
→ Projects quoted separately, so the monthly number stays predictable

The test for any provider: ask what happens when you DON'T use the hours.
If the answer isn't partial rollover plus a recommendation to downsize
when you're under 50% utilization two months running — they're selling
you prepaid labor, not coverage.

real cost math and the SLA terms to demand from anyone (including us).

## Reddit — r/msp (text post)

**Title:** How we structure Arizona field coverage for out-of-state MSPs —
and what I'd demand from ANY local partner

Disclosure up front: I run a field services company in Phoenix, so I'm on
the provider side of this. But the evaluation framework applies to any
local partner in any market, so posting it here.

The three ways MSPs cover markets where they have clients but no techs:

**1. Hire.** Fully-burdened field tech: $85K–$110K/yr. Works above ~30
recurring onsite hrs/week. Below that it's an expensive bet.

**2. Marketplace per-ticket.** Fine for true one-offs. Falls apart on
recurring work: new tech every ticket, no site memory, no docs trail,
no-show risk lands on YOUR SLA.

**3. Local partner on retainer.** The right call for 5+ clients in-market,
IF you vet them properly.

What to demand from any local partner (we lose deals on some of these,
and they're still the right asks):

- Published pricing or a rate card without a discovery call
- Photo documentation standard on every visit — not on request
- A written SLA with severity definitions, TWO clocks per severity
  (acknowledge + onsite), and automatic service credits
- White-label: your brand, your ticketing, their hands
- COI on request, $1M+ GL
- The same technicians returning to your sites
- Utilization reporting that recommends a DOWNGRADE when you're under
  50% usage two months running — this one filters out everyone selling
  prepaid hours instead of coverage

The last one is the tell. A partner who right-sizes you down is planning
to keep you for a decade.

Happy to answer questions about the provider side — what makes an MSP a
great (or nightmare) partner from where we sit.

## Reddit — r/datacenter (text post)

**Title:** The math on flying engineers to Phoenix vs. local smart hands —
and the SLA terms that actually matter

Provider disclosure: we do smart hands in the Phoenix market. The math
below is what we walk prospects through, and it cuts against us as often
as for us.

**When fly-in wins:** genuinely complex work needing YOUR engineer's
hands — major migrations, first-of-kind builds, anything where the runbook
doesn't exist yet. Fly them. Local hands can't replace institutional
knowledge.

**When it doesn't:** the $1,200 trip (flight + day rate + lost day) for a
30-minute cable swap, media rotation, or reboot-and-verify. Typical local
smart-hands pricing runs $150–$250 minimum per visit. If you're flying
someone quarterly for sub-2-hour tasks, you're paying 5–8x.

**Colo remote hands vs. third-party:** facility remote hands are fine for
"press the button" tickets but queue during YOUR maintenance window and
bill in 15–30 min increments at premium rates. Third-party smart hands
make sense when you need someone who has seen your specific racks before,
photo documentation as standard, or work across multiple facilities.

**What to put in any smart-hands agreement:**
- Two clocks per severity: time-to-acknowledge AND time-onsite
- Photo/work-log deliverable named in the contract, not "available"
- Escort and badging responsibilities defined per facility
- Rack elevation + port map updates included after physical changes —
  otherwise your documentation decays one ticket at a time
